## The molecule, in one sentence

A <Term id="peptide">peptide</Term> is a short chain of <Term id="amino-acid">amino acids</Term> linked by <Term id="peptide-bond">peptide bonds</Term> — the covalent bond formed when the carboxyl group (–COOH) of one amino acid joins the amino group (–NH₂) of the next, with a water molecule released in the process. That bond is what gives the entire molecule class its name.

That's it. Everything you'll learn in this course is downstream of "a chain of amino acids in a particular order."

## Where peptide ends and protein begins

The convention is loose, but useful:

- **Peptide:** roughly 2–50 amino acids
- **Protein:** ~50+ amino acids, typically folded into a stable 3D shape

The line is fuzzy on purpose — biology doesn't care about our categories. Insulin is 51 <Term id="residue">residues</Term> (one residue = one amino acid in the chain) and gets called both. Growth hormone at 191 is unambiguously a protein. BPC-157 at 15 is squarely a peptide.

<Callout type="key" title="Why size is the whole story">
Larger molecules fold into rigid 3D shapes that are harder to manufacture, harder to deliver, and more likely to trigger an immune response. Small enough to synthesize chemically, small enough to often slip past the immune system, big enough to have a specific receptor target — that's the peptide sweet spot. It's not a biological boundary, it's a manufacturing and delivery one.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="One amino acid. Billions of dollars.">
The FDA classifies any molecule at or under 40 amino acids as a **peptide** — one regulatory category, one set of rules. Anything above 40 is a **biologic** — harder for compounders to manufacture, longer market exclusivity (12 years vs. 5), much more profitable to hold the patent on.

Eli Lilly's retatrutide — a triple receptor agonist in late-stage clinical trials — is at the center of an active legal dispute over exactly this line. Lilly claims the molecule is 41 amino acids, which would make it a biologic: 12 years of exclusivity, compounding pharmacies blocked from producing it. The FDA's count puts it at 39 amino acids — a peptide. The two sides are now in litigation over the difference. One or two amino acids. Potentially billions in market exclusivity.

This is why the threshold isn't just a biology trivia question. It's a commercial battleground — and understanding it puts you ahead of most people discussing these compounds.

</Callout>

## Three flavors you'll meet in this course

When we talk about a peptide, it usually falls into one of three buckets — and the bucket tells you a lot about what to expect.

**1. <Term id="endogenous">Endogenous</Term> peptides** — molecules your body already makes. *Examples:* GHRH, ghrelin, glucagon, oxytocin. When you read about an "endogenous" molecule, the body has receptors and clearance mechanisms built specifically for it. They tend to have very short **half-lives** — the time it takes for half the peptide molecule to be cleared from the bloodstream — because the body wants tight, rapid control over these signals. We'll cover half-life in depth in Lesson 4, but hold onto the concept now: it determines how long a peptide stays active and how often it needs to be dosed.

**2. Analogs** — synthetic versions of endogenous peptides, modified for stability, potency, or duration. *Examples:* sermorelin (a GHRH analog), semaglutide (a GLP-1 analog), tesamorelin.

**Semaglutide is the clearest example in medicine right now.** Native GLP-1 — the hormone your gut releases after a meal to suppress appetite and regulate blood sugar — survives roughly 2 minutes in circulation before an enzyme destroys it. Semaglutide binds the exact same receptor, but was redesigned at the amino acid level to resist that enzyme, then modified to bind a blood protein that dramatically extends its lifespan. The result: a once-weekly injection instead of a molecule that wouldn't outlast a single meal. Same receptor. Completely different clinical reality.

That gap — endogenous vs. analog — is one of the most important things to understand about any peptide drug. The engineering choices explain the half-life, the dosing schedule, and many of the side effects.

**3. Synthetic / designed peptides** — molecules engineered to bind a target, sometimes inspired by nature, sometimes invented. *Examples:* BPC-157 (derived from a fragment of stomach protein but not endogenous in that form), the GHRPs.

Knowing which bucket a peptide sits in is the first thing to ask before reading anything else about it. It tells you whether the body has matching receptors waiting, whether the molecule was hand-tuned to evade clearance, and whether you should expect the effects to mirror a natural pathway or look like nothing the body has ever seen.

<Checkpoint number={1}>
<span slot="question">What's the practical difference between an endogenous peptide and an analog — and why would someone use the analog instead of the natural molecule?</span>

The endogenous version was built by evolution for tight, rapid control — it's released in a pulse and cleared in minutes so the body can regulate it precisely. An analog is engineered to resist breakdown, last longer, or bind more strongly. You'd choose the analog when you need a sustained effect the natural molecule can't deliver. Semaglutide vs. native GLP-1 is the clearest example: same receptor target, but the natural molecule lasts about 2 minutes while the analog lasts a week. The difference isn't the receptor — it's the engineering that determines how long the signal persists.
</Checkpoint>
