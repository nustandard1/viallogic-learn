A peptide circulating in your blood does nothing on its own. It has no power to break down fat, repair a tendon, or trigger growth hormone release. It needs to find a *doorway* — and the doorway is called a <Term id="receptor">receptor</Term>.

(Quick vocabulary note: any molecule that binds a receptor is called a <Term id="ligand">**ligand**</Term> — it's a generic word you'll see throughout the course. The peptide is the ligand; the receptor is its target.)

## Why peptides talk through the membrane, not across it

Peptides are too big and too charged to slip through a cell membrane. The membrane is a thin layer of fat (phospholipids) — small, fat-soluble molecules cross it easily, but a peptide carrying a string of *polar* (water-loving, charged) amino acids does not.

So peptide receptors live on the **cell surface**. One end of the receptor sticks out into the bloodstream (where the peptide can bind it). The other end faces inward (where it can talk to the cell's machinery). The peptide never enters the cell. It only flips a switch on the outside.

<Callout type="info" title="The lock-and-key picture is half right">
The classic textbook image is "lock and key" — the ligand fits the receptor like a key fits a lock. The truer picture is **induced fit**: the receptor and the ligand are both flexible, and binding involves a small mutual reshape. This matters because it explains how some molecules can bind a receptor without fully activating it. They fit the slot — but not perfectly enough to trigger the full conformational change. That's the seed of the agonist/antagonist distinction we'll cover in lesson 5.
</Callout>

## Two receptor families do nearly all the work

The peptide world is dominated by two architectures. Knowing which one a peptide targets tells you a lot about what to expect.

<ReceptorDiagram />

**<Term id="gpcr">GPCRs (G-protein coupled receptors)</Term>** — the largest family. When a peptide binds, the receptor changes shape slightly and activates an internal signaling cascade. About a third of all FDA-approved drugs target GPCRs. In the peptide world, this is where most of the action is: GLP-1, GHRH, GHRPs, somatostatin — most appetite, GH-axis, and metabolic peptides work here.

**<Term id="rtk">RTKs (receptor tyrosine kinases)</Term>** — a different architecture. Two receptor copies sit in the membrane; when a ligand bridges them, they activate each other and kick off a growth-promoting cascade. Insulin and IGF-1 are the canonical examples. This is the family behind long-term tissue growth, protein synthesis, and cell proliferation — when people say a hormone is "anabolic," this receptor family is usually involved downstream.

GPCRs and RTKs cover the vast majority of what you'll see in this course.

## Three terms you need to keep straight

You'll hear these constantly. They are not synonyms.

- <Term id="affinity">**Affinity**</Term> — how *tightly* a ligand binds. Measured by *Kd* or *EC50*. Lower number = tighter binding.
- <Term id="selectivity">**Selectivity**</Term> — how much it *prefers* one receptor over similar ones. Selective = hits one target. Promiscuous = hits several.
- <Term id="potency">**Potency**</Term> — how *little* drug you need for an effect. A function of both affinity *and* how efficiently the receptor signals once bound.

A drug can have high affinity for the wrong receptor (selective for trouble). A drug can have high potency without especially high affinity (a small amount triggers a strong cascade). When a manufacturer says a peptide is "more potent" than an alternative, the right question is: *for which receptor, and at what dose?*

<Checkpoint number={1}>
<span slot="question">What is the practical difference between affinity, selectivity, and potency — and why are they three different words?</span>

They describe three different things that are easy to conflate. **Affinity** is how tightly a ligand grips its receptor — a high-affinity compound holds on longer and at lower concentrations. **Selectivity** is whether it grips *that* receptor specifically, or whether it also grips several related ones — a selective peptide has predictable effects; a non-selective one may trigger receptors you didn't intend. **Potency** is how little of the drug you need to produce an effect — it depends on both affinity and how efficiently the receptor signals once bound. A drug can be high-affinity but hit the wrong receptor (affinity without selectivity). A drug can be low-affinity but still potent if the downstream cascade amplifies strongly. When someone says "more potent," the right question is: potent at which receptor, and at what dose?
</Checkpoint>
