Two terms worth a quick note before you scan the list:

**GHRH (growth hormone-releasing hormone)** — the hypothalamic peptide that tells the pituitary to release growth hormone. You'll see it referenced constantly in GH-axis discussions. It's the natural signal; sermorelin and CJC-1295 are analogs of it.

**GHRPs (growth hormone-releasing peptides)** — synthetic peptides that trigger GH release through a *different* receptor than GHRH. Ipamorelin, GHRP-2, and GHRP-6 are all GHRPs. The distinction matters because they're not the same as GHRH — they're synergistic with it. Both are covered fully in Modules 9 and 10.

## The whole module in one paragraph

A peptide molecule is a short chain of amino acids. It binds a receptor on the cell surface — almost always a GPCR or RTK, because peptides can't enter cells. The bound receptor triggers a signaling cascade (cAMP, calcium, or PI3K/Akt depending on the receptor) that amplifies the signal up to a million-fold inside the cell. Many cascades end at a **transcription factor** — a protein that enters the nucleus and switches specific genes on or off, which is how a surface binding event becomes a lasting change in what proteins the cell produces. Native peptides are cleared in minutes by enzymes like DPP-4, so therapeutic peptides are engineered (D-amino acids, lipidation, DAC) to extend half-life — at the cost of natural pulsatility. Receptors push back against persistent activation through phosphorylation, internalization, and downregulation, which is why protocols often cycle on and off. Whether a ligand activates the receptor fully, partially, blocks it, or biases it toward one downstream pathway is the difference between a clean peptide and a messy one. Everything else in this course is a special case of these ideas.
