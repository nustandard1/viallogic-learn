Most peptide research uses **U-100 insulin syringes** — small-gauge, widely available at any pharmacy, and precise at the volumes peptides require. The only friction: they measure in **units**, not mL, not mcg.

Understanding the conversion is the only arithmetic this field regularly requires.

<Callout type="info" title="Use a calculator — seriously">
There's no prize for doing this in your head. Write the numbers down, pull out your phone, whatever helps. The goal is to get the dose right, not to do mental math. This applies to the checkpoint below, the quiz, and the Dr. Harmon interview — if Harmon asks you a dosing question, he's happy to wait while you work through it on paper.
</Callout>

## What U-100 means

**U-100 = 100 units per mL.** That's it. The number refers to the syringe's scale, not any biological activity.

| Units | Volume |
|-------|--------|
| 100 | 1.0 mL |
| 50 | 0.5 mL |
| 20 | 0.2 mL |
| 10 | 0.1 mL |
| 5 | 0.05 mL |
| 1 | 0.01 mL |

<Callout type="caution" title="Units ≠ micrograms">
Insulin users think of "units" as a dose of insulin. In peptide research, units are purely a volume marker — 10 units = 0.1mL, regardless of what's in the syringe. The mcg you're delivering depends entirely on your vial's concentration. Same units, different concentration = different dose. Always recalculate when you change how a vial was reconstituted.
</Callout>

## The three-step calculation

**Step 1 — Concentration:** mcg/mL = total peptide (mcg) ÷ BAC water (mL)

**Step 2 — Volume:** mL needed = desired dose (mcg) ÷ concentration (mcg/mL)

**Step 3 — Units:** units to draw = mL × 100

<Callout type="key" title="Worked example — 250mcg BPC-157">
**Vial:** 5mg (5,000mcg), reconstituted with 2mL BAC water
**Concentration:** 5,000 ÷ 2 = 2,500 mcg/mL

**Desired dose:** 250mcg
**Volume needed:** 250 ÷ 2,500 = 0.1mL
**Units to draw:** 0.1 × 100 = **10 units**

Draw to the **10-unit line** on a U-100 syringe.
</Callout>

## A shortcut for repeated dosing

Once you know your concentration, calculate **mcg per unit** once and keep it handy:

**mcg per unit = concentration (mcg/mL) ÷ 100**

At 2,500 mcg/mL: 1 unit = 25mcg. So 250mcg = 10 units, 500mcg = 20 units. Simple mental math for the life of that vial.

<Callout type="key" title="Second worked example — 200mcg ipamorelin">
**Vial:** 10mg (10,000mcg), reconstituted with 2mL BAC water
**Concentration:** 10,000 ÷ 2 = 5,000 mcg/mL
**mcg per unit:** 5,000 ÷ 100 = 50mcg per unit

**Desired dose:** 200mcg
**Units to draw:** 200 ÷ 50 = **4 units**
</Callout>

## Putting the chain together

Vial size → add BAC water → concentration → desired dose ÷ concentration → mL → × 100 → units on the syringe.

That chain is the entire practical math of peptide dosing. The numbers change with every vial and every reconstitution volume, but the steps never do.

<span slot="question">You have a 2mg vial reconstituted with 1mL of BAC water. You want a 100mcg dose. How many units do you draw?</span>

Concentration: 2mg = 2,000mcg ÷ 1mL = 2,000 mcg/mL. Volume: 100 ÷ 2,000 = 0.05mL. Units: 0.05 × 100 = **5 units**. Shortcut: mcg per unit = 2,000 ÷ 100 = 20mcg/unit. 100mcg ÷ 20 = 5 units.
