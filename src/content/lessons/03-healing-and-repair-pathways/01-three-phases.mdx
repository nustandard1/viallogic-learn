Every injury — a torn tendon, a surgical incision, a muscle strain — goes through the same three-phase sequence. The phases overlap and transition gradually, but the order never changes. Most regenerative peptides work by accelerating or supporting one or more of these phases.

Understanding the sequence is the foundation. Once you have it, every compound-specific module becomes a matter of locating where the peptide plugs in.

## Phase 1 — Inflammatory (0 to ~72 hours)

Immediately after injury, the body triggers an inflammatory response. Blood vessels dilate and become leaky, allowing immune cells to flood the area. The result is the classic signs: redness, swelling, heat, pain.

This phase often gets treated as the enemy. It isn't. The inflammatory phase has three critical jobs:

- **Clears debris** — dead cells, damaged tissue, and pathogens are removed by immune cells (macrophages and neutrophils)
- **Releases growth factors** — the same immune cells deposit signaling proteins (VEGF, TGF-β, FGF) that trigger the next phase
- **Recruits repair cells** — signals go out to fibroblasts and stem cells to begin mobilizing toward the injury site

Blunt this phase too aggressively — with high-dose NSAIDs, corticosteroids, or even over-enthusiastic anti-inflammatory peptide use — and you remove the signals that kick off Phase 2. The injury may feel better sooner while actually healing slower.

<Callout type="caution" title="The NSAID timing problem">
NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) are appropriate for pain management, but using them aggressively in the first 48–72 hours suppresses prostaglandins — the same molecules that help coordinate the inflammatory response and initiate the proliferative phase. Short-term pain relief, potential cost to healing speed. The research on this is nuanced, but the takeaway is: early Phase 1 inflammation is doing real work.
</Callout>

## Phase 2 — Proliferative (days to weeks)

Once debris is cleared and growth factors are in place, the rebuild begins. The proliferative phase is when most of the biological construction happens:

- **Angiogenesis** — new blood vessels grow into the injury site, restoring oxygen and nutrient supply
- **Fibroblast activation** — specialized cells migrate to the site and begin producing collagen and other structural proteins
- **New tissue formation** — a preliminary scaffold of collagen fills the wound or injury gap

This is the phase most repair peptides target. BPC-157 accelerates angiogenesis and fibroblast activation. TB-500 enhances cell migration and proliferation. The proliferative phase is where the biological work of rebuilding happens — and it's also where poor blood supply, chronic inflammation, or inadequate growth factor signaling creates bottlenecks.

## Phase 3 — Remodeling (weeks to months)

The remodeling phase is the longest and least visible. The preliminary collagen scaffold laid down in Phase 2 is gradually reorganized and strengthened. Collagen fibers align along the lines of mechanical stress, cross-link for tensile strength, and shift from a weaker early form (Type III) to a stronger mature form (Type I).

This phase can last months — sometimes over a year for severe tendon injuries. During remodeling, the tissue may look healed and feel normal, but structural strength hasn't fully returned. This is why re-injury rates are high when people return to full load too early.

<Callout type="key" title="Why repair takes longer than it feels">
Pain and swelling resolve in Phase 2. Structural strength returns in Phase 3 — weeks to months later. The gap between "feels healed" and "is healed" is one of the most clinically important concepts in recovery, and one of the strongest arguments for protocols that extend support into Phase 3 (collagen synthesis, GHK-Cu, load management).
</Callout>
