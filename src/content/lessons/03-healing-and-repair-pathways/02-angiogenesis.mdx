Every time you read that a peptide "promotes angiogenesis," this is what's being claimed: the peptide stimulates the body to grow new blood vessels into damaged tissue.

That might sound like a minor detail. It's not. <Term id="angiogenesis">Angiogenesis</Term> is arguably the most important rate-limiting step in how quickly the proliferative phase can run — because without blood supply, everything else stalls.

## Why injured tissue loses its blood supply

When tissue is damaged — a torn tendon, a laceration, a stressed bone — the blood vessels supplying that area are damaged too. The area becomes hypoxic: starved of oxygen.

This creates a problem. Repair requires:
- **Oxygen** — for cellular energy and collagen synthesis
- **Nutrients** — amino acids, cofactors, building blocks for new tissue
- **Growth factors** — signaling proteins that tell fibroblasts to get to work
- **Immune cells** — to continue clearing debris and coordinating repair
- **Waste removal** — metabolic byproducts of active repair accumulate and need to be cleared

All of this is delivered by blood. Damaged tissue can't do any of it efficiently until blood supply is restored. That restoration is angiogenesis.

## What VEGF does

<Term id="vegf">VEGF</Term> (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor) is the primary molecular trigger for angiogenesis. Here's the sequence:

1. Hypoxic (oxygen-starved) tissue releases VEGF as a distress signal
2. VEGF binds receptors on endothelial cells — the cells that line blood vessel walls
3. Endothelial cells begin to proliferate and migrate toward the signal source
4. New capillary sprouts form and eventually connect into functional vessels
5. Blood flow to the injury site is restored

When a peptide is described as "upregulating VEGF" or "pro-angiogenic," it means the peptide is amplifying or accelerating step 1 — creating a stronger or earlier angiogenic signal that speeds up the formation of new vessels.

<Callout type="key" title="Why BPC-157's angiogenic effect matters">
BPC-157's most consistently documented mechanism is upregulation of the VEGF pathway and promotion of angiogenesis. In animal models of tendon, ligament, and muscle injury, one of the earliest measurable effects is faster restoration of blood supply to the injury site. This isn't a side effect or secondary mechanism — it's likely the primary reason for the accelerated healing observed. Better blood supply → faster delivery of everything repair requires → faster proliferative phase.
</Callout>

## Angiogenesis vs. vasodilation — a distinction worth knowing

These are often confused:

- **Vasodilation** — existing blood vessels widen, increasing blood flow. Immediate, temporary. This is what happens when you feel flushed or warm.
- **Angiogenesis** — new blood vessels are built. Slower, days to weeks, but creates durable new supply routes.

Many compounds affect vasodilation (nitric oxide, for example). Angiogenesis is a different and more structurally significant process — you're not widening what exists, you're building what doesn't exist yet.

<Checkpoint number={1}>
<span slot="question">You read that a peptide "promotes angiogenesis via the VEGF pathway." Put this in plain language — what is actually happening, and why does it matter for tissue injury?</span>

The peptide is stimulating the formation of new blood vessels at the injury site by increasing VEGF signaling. VEGF tells endothelial cells (the cells lining blood vessels) to grow and form new capillaries. This matters because injured tissue has compromised blood supply — the original vessels were damaged along with the tissue. Without restored blood flow, oxygen, nutrients, growth factors, and immune cells can't reach the repair site efficiently. Angiogenesis restores that supply chain. A peptide that accelerates it is removing one of the key bottlenecks in how fast the proliferative phase can run.
</Checkpoint>

Blood supply restores delivery. Now something has to actually do the building — and that's next.
