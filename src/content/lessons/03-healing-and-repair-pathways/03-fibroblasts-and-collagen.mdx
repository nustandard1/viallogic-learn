Once blood supply is restored and the inflammatory debris is cleared, the body starts the actual structural rebuild. Two things are central to this process: <Term id="fibroblast">fibroblasts</Term> — the cells that do the building — and <Term id="collagen">collagen</Term> — the structural protein they produce.

Most of what's marketed as "tissue repair" in the peptide world comes down to how well these two things are working.

## Fibroblasts — the construction workers

Fibroblasts are specialized cells whose primary job is to produce the structural proteins of connective tissue: collagen, elastin, and the extracellular matrix (the scaffold between cells). They're present in tendons, ligaments, skin, fascia, and most other structural tissues.

During the inflammatory phase, growth factors released by immune cells — particularly <Term id="tgf-b">TGF-β</Term> (Transforming Growth Factor-beta) and <Term id="fgf">FGF</Term> (Fibroblast Growth Factor) — act as the activation signal. Fibroblasts migrate to the injury site, proliferate, and shift into high-production mode.

When you read that a peptide "activates fibroblasts" or "upregulates TGF-β," this is the mechanism: the peptide is amplifying the signal that tells fibroblasts to show up and start building.

## Collagen — why repair takes time

Collagen is the most abundant protein in the body. It forms the structural framework of tendons, ligaments, skin, cartilage, and bone. What makes it relevant to understanding recovery timelines is how it's produced:

**Phase 2 (proliferative):** Fibroblasts rapidly deposit Type III collagen — a preliminary, loosely organized form that fills the structural gap quickly but has low tensile strength. This is what closes the wound or bridges the tear. It's fast. It's not strong.

**Phase 3 (remodeling):** Over weeks to months, Type III collagen is gradually broken down and replaced with Type I collagen — a more organized, cross-linked, structurally mature form. As fibers align along the lines of mechanical stress and cross-links form between strands, the tissue regains tensile strength.

<Callout type="key" title="The gap between feeling healed and being healed">
Type III collagen lays down fast — pain and swelling often resolve while it's being produced. Type I collagen takes weeks to months to mature. A tendon that feels fine and looks normal on imaging may still be predominantly Type III, with a fraction of its original tensile strength. This is the structural reason re-injury rates are high when people return to full load too early — and the same reason protocols that support collagen maturation (GHK-Cu, mechanical loading, adequate protein) extend well into what subjectively feels like recovery.
</Callout>

## Growth factors — the signals that coordinate everything

Several key signaling proteins orchestrate the fibroblast response. You'll see these names frequently:

- **TGF-β** — the primary driver of fibroblast activation and collagen synthesis. Also regulates scar formation. Higher TGF-β → more collagen, but also more scar tissue — the balance matters.
- **FGF** — stimulates fibroblast proliferation and migration; also supports angiogenesis
- **PDGF** (Platelet-Derived Growth Factor) — released from platelets at injury sites; recruits fibroblasts and stimulates their division

This is the same family of growth factors that PRP (platelet-rich plasma) concentrates — injecting a processed fraction of your own blood that's dense in these signals to kickstart Phase 2. The underlying logic is: more growth factor signal → more fibroblast activity → faster, more complete structural repair.

<Callout type="info" title="Where GHK-Cu fits">
GHK-Cu (the copper peptide in Module 8) works substantially through this pathway — it upregulates collagen and elastin synthesis, activates fibroblasts, and appears to shift the TGF-β balance away from scar-forming fibrosis and toward organized repair. Module 8 covers the mechanism in detail, but you now have the context for why those effects matter.
</Callout>

<Checkpoint number={1}>
<span slot="question">A tendon injury resolved weeks ago — no pain, full range of motion, cleared to return to sport. Three weeks later it tears again. What does the collagen maturation timeline suggest about what happened?</span>

Pain resolving and functional range of motion returning happen during Phase 2 — when Type III collagen (fast but weak) fills the structural gap. Type I collagen — the stronger, cross-linked, mature form — takes weeks to months to replace it. The tendon likely felt and functionally tested as healed while still being predominantly Type III collagen, with significantly less tensile strength than the original tissue. Returning to full sport load during this window is exactly the scenario where re-injury rates are highest. The structural rebuild wasn't complete even though the subjective and functional markers suggested otherwise.
</Checkpoint>

Blood supply and structural rebuild — those are the two pillars. But there's a third variable that can sabotage both: inflammation that doesn't resolve the way it's supposed to.
