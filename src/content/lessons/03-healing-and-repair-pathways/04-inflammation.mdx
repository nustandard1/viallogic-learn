"Anti-inflammatory" is used as a blanket positive in most peptide marketing. It isn't one. Whether reducing inflammation helps or hurts healing depends entirely on *which kind* of inflammation you're reducing and *when*.

The acute vs. chronic distinction is the most important conceptual split in this entire module — and the one most protocols get wrong.

## Acute inflammation — necessary, time-limited

Acute inflammation is the body's immediate response to injury or infection. It's characterized by:

- **Rapid onset** — minutes to hours after the triggering event
- **Classic signs** — redness, warmth, swelling, pain (all serve biological purposes)
- **Clear cause** — tissue damage, pathogen, physical stress
- **Time-limited** — designed to resolve in days once the job is done
- **Net effect: pro-healing** — coordinates the cascade that leads to repair

Acute inflammation is the Phase 1 we discussed in Lesson 1. Immune cells flood the area, <Term id="cytokine">cytokines</Term> are released, growth factors are deposited, and repair cells are recruited. Suppress it aggressively and you remove signaling the body needs to execute Phases 2 and 3.

## Chronic inflammation — persistent, destructive

Chronic inflammation is something different entirely. It's characterized by:

- **Persistent, low-grade** — no clear resolution, often present for months or years
- **No clear ongoing trigger** — or the trigger (like repeated microtrauma) never resolves
- **Ongoing tissue damage** — inflammatory mediators that were meant to be temporary become persistently elevated, breaking down tissue rather than building it
- **Net effect: anti-healing** — impairs fibroblast function, degrades collagen, blocks angiogenesis

Tendinopathy (the chronic pain and degeneration in tendons common in athletes) is largely a chronic inflammation problem. Inflammatory bowel conditions, arthritis, and metabolic syndrome all involve persistent inflammatory signaling that creates its own tissue damage separate from the original cause.

<Callout type="key" title="Where repair peptides fit in this picture">
BPC-157 and TB-500 both have anti-inflammatory properties — but what they're doing is modulating *chronic*, unresolved inflammation. They don't appear to blunt the acute inflammatory phase in the same way NSAIDs do. This is a meaningful distinction: resolving a stuck inflammatory state is pro-healing; blocking an active acute inflammatory response is not necessarily so.

This is why these peptides show healing effects in models of chronic injury while not appearing to impair acute repair.
</Callout>

## What gets stuck — and why

For inflammation to resolve, the body needs to actively switch from a pro-inflammatory to an anti-inflammatory state. Several things can prevent that switch:

- **Persistent mechanical stress** — the tissue keeps getting re-irritated before it heals (overuse injuries, poor load management)
- **Poor blood supply** — ischemic tissue can't clear inflammatory mediators efficiently
- **Metabolic dysfunction** — obesity, insulin resistance, and high blood sugar all create a systemic pro-inflammatory environment that interferes with local resolution
- **Age** — the resolution machinery becomes less efficient; chronic low-grade inflammation is increasingly recognized as a hallmark of aging ("inflammaging")

Understanding this is what makes the mitochondrial and metabolic modules (Module 13) relevant — cellular energy metabolism and inflammation are more tightly linked than most people recognize.

## The practical takeaway

When evaluating a peptide described as "anti-inflammatory," ask:
1. *Which type of inflammation does it target* — acute or chronic?
2. *At what phase of healing would it be used* — early acute, or weeks into a chronic problem?
3. *What's the mechanism* — does it block inflammatory enzymes broadly (like NSAIDs), or modulate specific inflammatory pathways?

A peptide that resolves chronic tendinopathy inflammation is pro-healing. A peptide that blunts acute Phase 1 inflammation could slow recovery. Same word, opposite outcomes.
