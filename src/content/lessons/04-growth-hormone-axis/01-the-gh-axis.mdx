Growth hormone doesn't come from one place and act on another. It's the output of a regulatory system — a cascade that starts in the brain, runs through the pituitary, and reaches every tissue in the body through two separate messengers: GH itself and, more importantly, <Term id="igf-1">IGF-1</Term>.

Understanding that system is understanding what every compound in Modules 9 and 10 actually does. None of them introduce GH into your body. They work on the signals upstream of GH — stimulating your own pituitary to release its own GH. That distinction matters.

## The axis, step by step

**Step 1 — The hypothalamus sends the signal**

The hypothalamus, a small structure at the base of the brain, governs the GH axis through two opposing peptides:

- <Term id="ghrh">GHRH</Term> (Growth Hormone-Releasing Hormone) — the stimulatory signal. GHRH is released in pulses and tells the pituitary to release GH.
- <Term id="somatostatin">Somatostatin</Term> — the inhibitory brake. Released when GH or IGF-1 rises too high, somatostatin blocks further GH secretion.

The balance between GHRH and somatostatin tone at any given moment determines how much GH the pituitary releases. This isn't a binary on/off switch — it's a continuous push and pull.

**Step 2 — The anterior pituitary responds**

GHRH travels a short distance to the <Term id="anterior-pituitary">anterior pituitary</Term>, where it binds receptors on specialized cells called <Term id="somatotroph">somatotrophs</Term>. These are the only cells in the body that produce growth hormone.

The GHRH receptor is a Gs-coupled GPCR — the same receptor type from Module 1. Binding GHRH activates adenylyl cyclase → <Term id="camp">cAMP</Term> rises → PKA activates → CREB is phosphorylated → the GH gene is switched on and stored GH vesicles are released by exocytosis. This is the cAMP pathway from Module 1 doing real work in a specific cell type.

**Step 3 — GH enters circulation**

GH (growth hormone itself) is a 191-amino-acid protein — too large to be called a peptide by FDA standards. It enters the bloodstream and acts on two types of targets:

- **Fat cells (adipocytes)** — directly, producing <Term id="lipolysis">lipolysis</Term> (breakdown of stored fat into free fatty acids). This is a direct GH effect; it does not require IGF-1 as an intermediary.
- **The liver** — GH binds receptors on liver cells and signals them to produce and secrete IGF-1.

**Step 4 — The liver produces IGF-1**

This is the step most people skip, and it's the most important one. The liver responds to GH by producing <Term id="igf-1">IGF-1</Term> (Insulin-like Growth Factor 1) and releasing it into circulation. IGF-1 then acts on virtually every tissue in the body.

Most of what gets attributed to "GH" — muscle growth, collagen synthesis, cell proliferation — is actually IGF-1 driving those effects downstream. GH is the signal to the liver. IGF-1 is the molecule that does most of the building.

**Step 5 — Negative feedback closes the loop**

As GH and IGF-1 rise, they feed back to the hypothalamus:

- Elevated GH and IGF-1 reduce GHRH release
- Elevated IGF-1 increases somatostatin tone

This <Term id="negative-feedback">negative feedback</Term> is what keeps the axis in balance. It's also why continuously elevated GH — from exogenous injections or very long-acting GHRH analogs — can suppress the body's own signaling over time. The axis interprets the elevated GH/IGF-1 as a signal that it doesn't need to fire.

<Callout type="key" title="GHRH analogs don't replace GH — they stimulate your pituitary to release its own">
Sermorelin, CJC-1295, and tesamorelin are all GHRH analogs. When you use them, you're not putting GH into your body — you're sending a signal to your pituitary that mimics what the hypothalamus does naturally. The pituitary then releases your own endogenous GH in a pulse. The anabolic and lipolytic effects come from that endogenous GH and the IGF-1 it triggers. This is a fundamentally different pharmacological approach from exogenous recombinant GH, which bypasses the pituitary entirely.
</Callout>

## Why the axis exists

The GH axis isn't just a delivery mechanism. It's a regulatory one. The hypothalamus adjusts GHRH and somatostatin based on inputs like sleep, fasting, exercise, blood glucose, and circulating IGF-1. The result is that GH output is tuned to the body's metabolic state — high during growth phases, sleep, and caloric deficit; suppressed after meals (especially carbohydrate-rich ones) and during states of high IGF-1.

Peptides that work at Step 1 (GHRH analogs) or add a second signal (GHRPs — covered in Lesson 4) are working with this regulatory logic. Exogenous GH bypasses it. That distinction shapes everything about the safety profile, the feedback behavior, and the long-term axis health of each approach.
