## The whole module in one paragraph

The GH axis runs from the hypothalamus (GHRH stimulates; somatostatin inhibits) to the anterior pituitary somatotrophs (which release GH in pulses via cAMP-mediated GHRH receptor signaling) to the liver (which produces IGF-1 in response to GH) to every anabolic target in the body (muscle, bone, connective tissue — driven by IGF-1's RTK/PI3K/Akt/mTOR cascade). GH itself drives lipolysis directly; most of its growth effects require IGF-1 as an intermediary, which is why serum IGF-1 is the practical proxy for axis activity. GH is released in pulses — not continuously — and that pattern matters: pulse amplitude drives IGF-1 response, troughs preserve receptor sensitivity, and disrupting pulsatility with long-acting analogs trades convenience for blunted long-term axis responsiveness. GHRPs are ghrelin mimetics that bind a completely separate receptor (GHS-R1a, Gq-coupled) from GHRH analogs (Gs-coupled GHRH receptor) — the two signals are complementary, producing multiplicative GH release when combined. Negative feedback closes the loop: elevated GH and IGF-1 reduce GHRH and increase somatostatin, which is why exogenous GH can suppress endogenous axis activity over time.
