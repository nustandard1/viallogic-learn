The majority of what people know about peptides came from a post, a caption, a YouTube video, or a forum thread. Not a journal. Not a textbook. Not a conversation with a researcher. This is the reality of how information spreads — and it means that by the time a finding reaches most people, it has typically passed through several layers of interpretation, each adding a little more certainty and a little less nuance than the source actually warranted.

The skill this lesson teaches is not cynicism. It's triage: being able to look at a claim and immediately categorize what type of claim it is, whether it's traceable to a source, and what that source actually says.

## How a finding travels

Start with a real research event: a lab publishes a rodent study showing that BPC-157 injections accelerated tendon repair compared to a saline control. The study is in rats. The dose is high relative to typical human use. The paper is titled something like "Gastric pentadecapeptide BPC-157 counteracts clostridium difficile toxin B-caused effects in mice."

Here's what happens next:

A science journalist summarizes it: *"New research suggests BPC-157 may support tendon healing."*

A health blogger picks it up: *"Studies confirm BPC-157 heals tendons and ligaments."*

A supplement company cites the blogger in their product description: *"Clinically studied for tissue repair."*

An influencer reads the product page: *"BPC-157 is literally proven to heal your tendons faster — doctors won't tell you about this."*

Their follower reposts it: *"BPC-157 heals injuries. Period."*

At no point did anyone lie with intent to deceive. But a conditional rodent finding became a clinical certainty through five rounds of telephone. This is the normal lifecycle of health information online. It doesn't require bad faith — it just requires that each link in the chain emphasizes the interesting part and drops the qualifications.

## Red flag phrases

These phrases appear constantly in peptide content. Each one warrants a specific follow-up question.

**"Studies show…"**
Which studies? What kind? Where can I read them? This phrase carries authority without earning it. The follow-up is always: *what study, and can I see it?*

**"Proven to…"**
Proven in what population, under what conditions, at what dose? "Proven" implies an RCT-level certainty that almost no research peptide has. When you see this word, downgrade your confidence in the source.

**"Clinically studied" or "research-backed"**
These phrases technically require only that *some* study *somewhere* referenced the compound. A rat study counts. An in vitro experiment counts. They do not imply human clinical trials.

**"Doctors don't want you to know" / "Big Pharma suppresses this"**
This framing is a narrative device, not evidence. It pre-immunizes a claim against scrutiny: any challenge can be dismissed as part of the conspiracy. It's worth asking why a compound that "works" would need this framing at all.

**"I tried this for 30 days and…"**
An n=1 uncontrolled observation. The person may genuinely feel better. But they also changed their diet, started sleeping more, reduced stress, and experienced a natural recovery cycle — none of which can be controlled for. Personal experience is real. It's not transferable evidence.

**"10x more anabolic than…" / "works better than steroids without the side effects"**
Comparative superlatives require comparative studies. These almost never exist for research peptides. The claim usually originated from a speculative forum post and was amplified without scrutiny.

<Callout type="warning" title="Absence of a study is not the same as absence of evidence">
Don't overcorrect into blanket dismissal. "There's no human RCT" does not mean "this doesn't work." It means the confidence level is lower than an established drug. Some compounds with strong mechanistic and animal evidence and a large anecdotal base are plausibly effective even without formal trials. The honest position is calibrated uncertainty — not endorsement, not dismissal.
</Callout>

## The incentive problem

People who create peptide content online usually have a financial relationship with the outcome. This isn't necessarily corruption — but it's a systematic pressure that affects what gets shared and how it gets framed.

**Affiliate links.** A creator sends you to a vendor. If you buy, they earn 10–30% commission. The incentive is for you to buy, not for you to have a nuanced understanding of the evidence.

**Vendor sponsorships.** Some creators are paid directly by suppliers. Their content is effectively advertising, regardless of how it's labeled.

**Course and community sales.** Creators build authority by appearing to know more than you. Hedging — "this might work but the evidence is limited" — undermines perceived authority. Confident claims build audiences.

**Genuine belief.** Many creators genuinely experienced something they attribute to a compound and want to share it. This doesn't make their interpretation of why it worked accurate.

None of this means all content creators are dishonest. It means the incentive structure systematically favors confident claims over accurate ones, and you should weight what you see accordingly.

## A framework for evaluating any claim

When you encounter a peptide claim — post, video, article, forum thread — run through these five questions:

**1. Is a specific source cited?**
If yes, find it. If no, that's already informative. PubMed (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) is free and searchable. Most legitimate research will appear there.

**2. What type of study is it?**
In vitro, animal, or human? If animal or human — how many subjects? In what population?

**3. What was the actual finding vs. how it's being presented?**
Read the abstract. Does the source say what the claim says it says? You'll often find the original finding was conditional, limited, or about a different endpoint entirely.

**4. What dose was used?**
Research peptide doses in studies often don't correspond to typical use cases. A dose that produces an effect in a 25g rat at 10mg/kg does not automatically translate to a meaningful human dose.

**5. Does this person have a financial incentive to make this claim?**
Not disqualifying — but a relevant prior. Sponsored content and affiliate-linked recommendations deserve additional scrutiny.

<Callout type="info" title="How to find the actual study">
If a claim includes something like "researchers found that BPC-157 increased tendon strength by 40%," search PubMed for: `BPC-157 tendon`. Skim the titles. Click the most relevant result. Read the abstract — it's free. Check the species, the sample size, and the actual measured outcome. Takes two minutes and instantly tells you whether the claim matches the source.
</Callout>

## What good content actually looks like

The best peptide content online — and there is some — tends to share a few characteristics:

- Links to the actual primary source (the paper), not to a news article about a paper
- Uses language that tracks the evidence tier: "in rodent models," "mechanistically plausible," "in early human studies"
- Acknowledges what isn't known alongside what is
- Doesn't require you to believe that someone powerful is hiding the truth
- Updates when new evidence changes the picture

You can learn to recognize this style quickly, and when you see it, it's a meaningful positive signal about the source's reliability.

<span slot="question">You see an Instagram post that reads: "BPC-157 is clinically proven to regenerate cartilage. Studies show it works better than any drug available. Tap the link for our premium research-grade supply." Identify at least three specific problems with this claim and explain what you'd do to verify it.</span>

Problems: (1) "Clinically proven" implies human RCT evidence — BPC-157 has no published human clinical trials; the evidence base is animal models and in vitro. (2) "Studies show it works better than any drug" is a comparative superlative with no cited source — no head-to-head comparison study exists. (3) The post links directly to a vendor, creating a financial incentive that compromises the claim's credibility. Verification steps: search PubMed for "BPC-157 cartilage" to find actual studies and check their design (animal or human, sample size, methodology); note whether any human RCT appears; check the claim against the hierarchy — strong animal data exists, but "clinically proven" overstates it. The vendor link should increase skepticism, not trigger a purchase.
</Checkpoint>

You now have three connected tools: the hierarchy tells you what type of evidence exists, critical reading tells you whether that evidence is sound, and this framework tells you whether a real study is even behind the claim at all. The last piece of the evaluation puzzle is product quality — which is where COAs come in.
