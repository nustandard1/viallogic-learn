BPC-157's primary mechanism comes down to one enzyme: <Term id="enos">eNOS</Term> — endothelial nitric oxide synthase. BPC-157 upregulates it. Understanding what eNOS does is what the rest of this lesson is about.

<Callout type="key" title="The core sequence">
**BPC-157 → eNOS upregulated in endothelial cells → more NO produced**

Two effects run from there:

**→ Vasodilation:** NO relaxes the smooth muscle surrounding blood vessels → vessels widen → more blood, oxygen, and nutrients reach the injury site immediately.

**→ Angiogenesis:** elevated eNOS amplifies the VEGF signaling cascade → new capillaries grow toward oxygen-starved tissue.

Both converge: damaged tissue gets more blood supply → repair cells arrive, structural rebuilding accelerates → faster healing.

The details in this lesson fill in the steps between these arrows. Keep this chain in mind as they do.
</Callout>

## The isoform distinction that actually matters

<Term id="nitric-oxide">Nitric oxide</Term> is produced by a family of enzymes called nitric oxide synthases (NOS). There are three, and they don't do the same thing. The two that matter here:

- **iNOS** (inducible) — the inflammatory version. Immune cells express it during acute inflammation, producing high-output NO as part of the immune response.
- **eNOS** (endothelial) — the vascular version. Expressed in the cells lining blood vessel walls, it produces lower-volume, regulated NO that drives blood vessel function and repair.

This distinction is why "works through the nitric oxide system" is almost meaningless without knowing which isoform. Suppressing iNOS is anti-inflammatory — you're reducing the inflammatory signal. Upregulating eNOS is pro-vascular and pro-repair — you're increasing the blood vessels' capacity to do their job.

BPC-157 targets eNOS. It is not an anti-inflammatory compound.

## What eNOS-derived NO actually does

**Vasodilation.** NO produced by endothelial cells diffuses into the surrounding smooth muscle, which causes it to relax — the vessel widens and more blood flows through.

If you've ever taken an L-citrulline-based preworkout and noticed a pump during training, you've felt this mechanism. L-citrulline converts to arginine in the body; arginine is what eNOS uses to make NO; NO relaxes smooth muscle; blood vessels dilate. BPC-157 drives the same process — but instead of supplying more substrate (arginine), it upregulates eNOS itself, increasing the endothelial cells' capacity to produce NO in the first place.

For injured tissue, this matters immediately: more blood flow means more oxygen, nutrients, and repair cells delivered to the site — without waiting for new vessels to form.

**Angiogenesis — new blood vessel growth.** Your body has a dedicated signal for when tissue is damaged or oxygen-starved and needs new blood vessels: VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor). When VEGF is released at an injury site, it tells nearby endothelial cells to multiply and migrate toward the oxygen-starved zone, forming new capillaries.

NO is how VEGF carries out that instruction. VEGF binds endothelial cells and triggers a cascade that ends with eNOS activation and NO production — which then drives endothelial cell proliferation and the formation of new capillaries.

When BPC-157 upregulates eNOS, it's priming that cascade. The endothelial cells can produce more NO when VEGF calls for it, which produces a stronger angiogenic response — more new vessels, faster.

Vasodilation and angiogenesis aren't two separate effects. They're the same mechanism at different timescales: NO improves blood flow through existing vessels immediately, and NO is also the signal that grows new ones.

**Cell survival under stress.** At the concentrations eNOS produces, NO also protects cells from dying during ischemia (oxygen deprivation) and mechanical trauma. This is one reason BPC-157 shows up in CNS injury and gut damage research — wherever the eNOS/NO pathway operates, cells under stress survive longer.

<Callout type="key" title="The L-NAME proof">
L-NAME is a compound that blocks all NOS isoforms — it shuts down NO production throughout the system. Researchers co-administered L-NAME with BPC-157 in injury models.

BPC-157's healing acceleration largely disappeared.

This is the pharmacological proof that NO is the primary downstream signal. The logic is clean: demonstrate the effect → block the proposed pathway → confirm the effect is gone. When eliminating NO production eliminates the compound's benefit, that's strong evidence NO is carrying the signal, not just correlating with it. This kind of blockade experiment is among the best mechanistic evidence available in animal research.
</Callout>

## Why this is not "anti-inflammatory"

BPC-157 is often described as anti-inflammatory. The confusion comes from iNOS: because high-output inflammatory NO can be harmful in excess, compounds that suppress iNOS get labeled anti-inflammatory. BPC-157 doesn't work that way.

eNOS upregulation doesn't suppress the immune response. It accelerates the repair processes that resolve the conditions causing inflammation in the first place. When tissue heals faster, inflammation resolves faster — but the reduced inflammation is a downstream consequence, not the mechanism.

This matters for timing. Anti-inflammatory agents used immediately after injury can blunt the Phase 1 signaling that kicks off repair — the NSAID problem from Module 3. A compound working through eNOS isn't interfering with Phase 1 at all. It's accelerating Phase 2.

<Callout type="info" title="Why BPC-157 shows effects across so many tissues">
eNOS is expressed in the endothelial cells lining every blood vessel in the body — tendons, gut, brain, muscle, everywhere. When BPC-157 upregulates eNOS, it's not targeting a specific organ. It's targeting the cell type that's universally present wherever vasculature exists. This is the most coherent explanation for why one mechanism produces effects across such different tissues.
</Callout>
