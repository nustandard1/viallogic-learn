## The whole module in one paragraph

BPC-157 is a synthetic 15-amino acid peptide derived from a sequence found in human gastric protein — not a freely circulating endogenous hormone, and not primarily anti-inflammatory despite common framing. Its primary mechanism is eNOS upregulation in endothelial cells: increased eNOS expression drives vasodilation, amplifies the VEGF-mediated angiogenic cascade (VEGF → PI3K → Akt → eNOS → NO → endothelial proliferation and migration → new capillaries), and confers cytoprotection by blocking the apoptotic caspase cascade. The L-NAME experiment — showing that pan-NOS inhibition largely abolishes BPC-157's healing effects — is the pharmacological proof that NO is the primary downstream carrier. Tendons are the most meaningful test case: poor baseline vascularity makes angiogenesis the rate-limiting repair step, sparse tenocytes slow collagen deposition, and BPC-157 targets both directly — matching mechanism to vulnerability. Systemic effects from a short-half-life peptide are explained by cascade amplification (a small amount triggers a large downstream event) and NO propagation (eNOS-derived NO diffuses systemically without the peptide itself needing to travel). CNS effects — GABAergic modulation in withdrawal models, bidirectional dopaminergic normalization, neuroprotection via cerebral vascular eNOS — follow from the fact that eNOS is expressed wherever vasculature exists, including the brain and spinal cord. The evidence base is 200+ animal studies from a single research group; human clinical evidence for musculoskeletal applications from completed RCTs is absent.
