## The module in one paragraph

BPC-157 is a synthetic 15-amino acid peptide derived from a sequence in human gastric protein — it is not endogenous in the meaningful sense, and does not have a confirmed receptor. It engages multiple downstream systems through what appear to be indirect interactions with Src kinase family members: eNOS upregulation via two parallel routes (VEGFR2/PI3K/Akt and Src-Caveolin-1 directly), VEGF upregulation driving angiogenesis, and FAK-paxillin pathway activation driving fibroblast and tenocyte migration and proliferation. The L-NAME proof — blocking all NOS isoforms largely abolishes BPC-157's healing effects in animal models — confirms NO as a primary downstream mediator. In tendon tissue specifically, these three mechanisms directly address the two structural repair bottlenecks: poor vascularity (addressed by eNOS/VEGF/angiogenesis) and sparse tenocyte population (addressed by FAK + growth factor upregulation, including a notable 7-fold increase in growth hormone receptor expression in tendon fibroblasts). Despite a plasma half-life under 30 minutes, lasting effects are explained by the biological switch model: BPC-157 initiates self-sustaining gene expression programs that continue after the peptide clears. Phase 2 IBD trials occurred in the early 2000s and did not progress to Phase 3, most likely due to corporate acquisition of Pliva — the compound was never failed in trials. For musculoskeletal applications, there are no completed human RCTs. The angiogenesis-cancer question is unresolved and warrants honest acknowledgment.
