"TB-500" is the research peptide name for a synthetic version of a specific fragment of Thymosin Beta-4 (TB4). Not the full protein — a 7-amino acid segment from its interior: **Ac-LKKTETQ** (residues 17–23 of the 43-amino acid Tβ4 sequence). This segment contains Tβ4's actin-binding motif — the functional core of the actin sequestration mechanism.

That's the scientific definition. Published analytical chemistry and doping-control research (Esposito et al. 2012; Thevis et al. 2013) identified and characterized TB-500 as Ac-LKKTETQ. WADA tracks it as this fragment. Its international nonproprietary name is *fequesetide*.

<Callout type="caution" title="The market naming problem">
In practice, the research peptide market uses "TB-500" loosely. Many vendors sell the full 43-amino acid synthetic Tβ4 protein under the TB-500 label. When you see TB-500 referenced online or in discussion forums, the compound being discussed may be the 7-amino acid fragment or the full 43-amino acid protein — and the person writing probably doesn't know which.

This matters for reading the evidence: almost all published efficacy data (cardiac protection, wound healing, stem cell mobilization) is for full-length Tβ4, not the fragment. When someone cites "studies on TB-500," they're almost certainly citing Tβ4 research and assuming the fragment produces equivalent effects. That assumption is plausible but is an extrapolation — not a direct confirmation.
</Callout>

## The parent protein: Thymosin Beta-4

To understand TB-500, you need to understand what it was derived from. Thymosin Beta-4 (Tβ4) is a 43-amino acid peptide that is genuinely endogenous — produced by cells throughout the body, regulated, and used in normal biological signaling. It is one of the most abundant intracellular peptides in virtually all mammalian cells, particularly platelets and white blood cells.

**TB-500 (Ac-LKKTETQ) is not itself endogenous as a free-floating peptide.** It is a sequence buried inside Tβ4 — not something the body produces or releases in isolation. The parent protein is endogenous; the isolated fragment is a synthetic research construct.

This is a meaningful distinction from BPC-157, where the debate is about whether the term "endogenous" applies at all. Here, it applies clearly to the parent protein — Tβ4 is genuinely endogenous — but it does not automatically transfer to the fragment sold as TB-500. Ac-LKKTETQ has not been identified as a naturally circulating free peptide. Some cleavage of Tβ4 may produce it in vivo, but that's different from it being a regulated endogenous signal.

What the Tβ4 connection does provide:
- **Mechanistic credibility**: Tβ4 has decades of research behind it. The fragment's mechanism is grounded in that biology
- **Lower immune recognition risk**: Ac-LKKTETQ is derived from a human protein sequence — less likely to be recognized as foreign than a fully novel sequence
- **A richer evidence context**: understanding Tβ4's effects informs what the fragment might do — with the caveat that those effects were demonstrated for the full protein, not the fragment specifically

## The actin-binding motif

The reason residues 17–23 were identified as the active fragment is that LKKTETQ is the actin-binding motif of Tβ4. Research has shown this sequence is essential for Tβ4's actin sequestration activity — peptides lacking any portion of it are largely inactive. Studies have demonstrated that the isolated 7-amino acid fragment shows near-identical actin-binding and angiogenic activity to the full-length protein at similar concentrations, which is the mechanistic basis for TB-500's proposed equivalence to Tβ4.

The fragment captures the core functional mechanism. Whether it also produces all of the broader effects attributed to full-length Tβ4 — stem cell mobilization, cardiac protection — is less established. Those effects may depend on parts of the full protein beyond the actin-binding domain.

## The research landscape

The published research on Tβ4 covers cardiac protection, wound healing, corneal repair, and musculoskeletal injury. This is the evidence base that TB-500 discussions draw on. A few things to hold onto when reading it:

- Studies typically used recombinant full-length Tβ4, not the synthetic fragment
- The extrapolation from Tβ4 research to TB-500 the fragment is mechanistically plausible for actin-related effects; it is less certain for effects that may require the full protein
- The cardiac and wound healing evidence is strongest; musculoskeletal evidence is thinner and less replicated
- No completed human RCTs exist for musculoskeletal applications of either Tβ4 or TB-500

<Callout type="key" title="How to hold the TB-500 / Tβ4 evidence relationship">
When someone cites evidence for TB-500, they are almost always citing Tβ4 research. The mechanism transfer is plausible — the fragment contains the active domain — but the evidence was generated for the full protein. Treat TB-500's evidence base as "strong mechanistic confidence, Tβ4 efficacy data, and an extrapolation gap between the two."
</Callout>
