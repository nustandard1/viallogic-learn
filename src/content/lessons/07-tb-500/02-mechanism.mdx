Actin is the most abundant protein in most animal cells — so abundant that understanding what it does and how it's regulated is foundational to understanding cell behavior. TB-500's primary mechanism runs entirely through actin biology.

## G-actin and F-actin: the two states of actin

Actin exists in two interconvertible forms:

**<Term id="g-actin">G-actin</Term>** (globular actin) is the monomer — a single folded protein unit, roughly spherical, floating free in the cell's cytoplasm. G-actin is the unpolymerized form: not yet assembled into anything structural. It's the raw material.

**<Term id="f-actin">F-actin</Term>** (filamentous actin) is the polymer — G-actin monomers assembled end-to-end into long, stiff filaments. F-actin filaments are structural. They form networks that give cells their shape, provide mechanical resistance, and — critically for repair — power cell movement.

The ratio of G-actin to F-actin in a cell is tightly regulated because it determines what the cell can do:

- **High F-actin:** cell is making structural scaffolding, extending projections, actively moving
- **High G-actin pool (more free monomers):** potential energy for rapid polymerization on demand

The conversion goes in both directions constantly. Cells build new F-actin at the leading edge when they move and tear it down at the trailing edge. The actin cytoskeleton is not a static structure — it's a dynamic system being continuously remodeled.

## How Tβ4 regulates this system

Tβ4 binds to <Term id="g-actin">G-actin</Term> monomers in a 1:1 complex. This is called **actin sequestration** — Tβ4 holds G-actin monomers in reserve, preventing them from spontaneously polymerizing into filaments.

This sounds like it would inhibit actin function, but the biology is more nuanced. Tβ4 acts as a buffer:

- It maintains a large, rapidly available pool of sequestered G-actin
- When the cell needs to build F-actin quickly — for example, to extend a migratory projection — Tβ4 releases G-actin monomers on demand
- The size of the sequestered pool determines how fast and how aggressively a cell can reorganize its cytoskeleton

Think of it as a ready reserve. A cell with a large Tβ4-sequestered G-actin pool can mount a faster, more coordinated migratory response than one without it — because the monomers are right there, buffered and available, rather than having to be generated from scratch.

<Callout type="info" title="Why Tβ4 is found in such high concentrations">
Tβ4 exists at roughly 0.4 mM in platelets — one of the highest concentrations of any intracellular peptide. This makes sense given the function: platelets are among the first cells deployed to injury sites and must move and aggregate rapidly. A large sequestered G-actin pool means platelets can reorganize their cytoskeleton and initiate clot formation quickly. High Tβ4 concentrations in platelets are a feature, not a coincidence.
</Callout>

## Cell motility: why this matters for tissue repair

When tissue is injured, repair requires cells to move — fibroblasts migrating toward the wound, endothelial cells forming new vessels, immune cells homing to the injury site. Cell migration is driven by actin dynamics: the cell extends projections at its front (by polymerizing F-actin), anchors them, and pulls itself forward while retracting its tail (by depolymerizing F-actin at the rear).

Tβ4 regulates this process by controlling the available G-actin pool. Studies on dermal fibroblasts and endothelial cells show that Tβ4 accelerates migration rate and increases the speed and organization of wound closure — effects directly attributable to enhanced actin dynamics.

This is the cell-level mechanism underlying TB-500's documented healing effects. It's not acting on a receptor that triggers a signaling cascade. It's modulating the internal machinery that cells use to physically move through tissue.

## Stem cell mobilization

Beyond cell motility in existing tissue, Tβ4 promotes the mobilization of <Term id="progenitor-cell">progenitor cells</Term> — early-stage cells capable of differentiating into tissue-specific repair cells — from the bone marrow into peripheral circulation. Once in circulation, these cells can home to injury signals (particularly inflammatory cytokines and growth factors released at damage sites).

This is a fundamentally different recruitment mechanism than BPC-157 uses. BPC-157 activates fibroblasts and tenocytes that are already at the injury site. TB-500 calls in reinforcements from circulation. Both contribute to tissue repair; they're addressing the supply problem at different levels.

The stem cell mobilization effect is one of the primary reasons TB-500's distribution matters — a compound mobilizing circulating progenitors needs to be systemic, which is exactly what Tβ4 is.

## Anti-inflammatory effects via NF-κB

<Term id="nf-kb">NF-κB</Term> (nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells) is the master transcription factor for inflammatory gene expression. When activated, it drives production of pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, and others. Chronic inflammation involves persistent NF-κB activation — the signal for resolution never arrives.

Tβ4 has been shown to downregulate NF-κB signaling, reducing pro-inflammatory cytokine production. This anti-inflammatory effect is secondary to the actin/motility mechanism in terms of TB-500's primary identity as a repair compound — but it contributes meaningfully to the overall profile. Reduced inflammatory burden at an injury site lets the repair phase proceed without the ongoing damage that chronic inflammation produces.

<Callout type="key" title="What makes this mechanism distinct">
TB-500's mechanism is upstream of most peptide mechanisms covered in this course. Most peptides bind a cell-surface receptor and trigger intracellular signaling cascades. Tβ4 enters cells (or is produced intracellularly) and directly interacts with the actin pool — modulating the physical machinery of cell function rather than sending a message to it.

This is relevant to the "no confirmed receptor" issue: Tβ4 doesn't primarily work through a receptor in the classical sense. Its actin-binding activity is a direct protein-protein interaction. Some extracellular signaling through cell surface interactions has been proposed and studied, but the primary mechanism is intracellular actin regulation.
</Callout>
