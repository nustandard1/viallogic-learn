GHK-Cu stands for Glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine copper complex — a tripeptide (three amino acids: glycine, histidine, lysine) chelated to a copper ion (Cu²⁺). It is one of the simplest compounds in this course by structure and one of the most mechanistically distinct by how it works.

## Origin: genuinely endogenous, and from a specific source

GHK is found naturally in human plasma, saliva, and urine. It is genuinely endogenous — not just a sequence derived from a human protein (like BPC-157), but a peptide that actually circulates in the body at measurable concentrations. Its source has been identified: GHK is released by proteolytic cleavage of **SPARC** (Secreted Protein Acidic and Rich in Cysteine, also called osteonectin), a protein involved in extracellular matrix organization and tissue remodeling. When SPARC is cleaved during normal ECM turnover and tissue injury, GHK is among the released fragments.

This origin matters for understanding when GHK is produced: it's released as part of the tissue remodeling process itself — a signal embedded in the machinery of injury response, not a hormone secreted from a distant gland.

## GHK vs. GHK-Cu: an important distinction

These are not the same compound.

**GHK** is the free tripeptide. It has some independent biological activity — including quenching lipid peroxidation byproducts — but most of its documented effects require copper.

**GHK-Cu** is the 1:1 chelated complex: one GHK peptide bound to one Cu²⁺ ion. This is the primary bioactive form for wound healing, collagen synthesis, and most of the effects this module covers. The research literature sometimes uses the names interchangeably, which creates confusion. When reading a GHK study, checking which form was actually used matters — the results may not transfer between them.

<Callout type="info" title="How the chelation forms">
The copper binds primarily through the histidine imidazole nitrogen — that's the anchor point. At physiological pH, the glycine amino group and the peptide backbone also participate in coordinating the copper ion. The lysine side chain does not significantly participate in copper binding at pH 7.4, which means it remains available for other biological interactions. The result is a stable complex where Cu²⁺ is held tightly enough to prevent free-radical generation, but bioavailable enough to be delivered to copper-requiring enzymes.
</Callout>

## A mechanistically different compound

Every compound covered so far in this course works primarily through receptor interaction or direct protein binding:
- BPC-157 engages Src kinase family members and drives downstream signaling cascades
- TB-500 (Ac-LKKTETQ) directly binds G-actin monomers

GHK-Cu works differently. Its two proposed primary mechanisms are:
1. **Delivering copper** to enzymes that require it as a cofactor — enabling reactions those enzymes couldn't complete otherwise
2. **Modulating gene expression** broadly — influencing which genes cells actively transcribe

Neither mechanism involves binding a receptor and triggering a cascade in the conventional sense. This is why GHK-Cu produces effects across such a wide range of tissues: it's not targeting a specific receptor population, it's addressing a fundamental requirement (copper availability) and influencing transcriptional programs that many cell types share.

<Callout type="key" title="The framing shift this compound requires">
With BPC-157 and TB-500, the question was: "what does this compound bind, and what does that binding set in motion?" With GHK-Cu, the better question is: "what enzymatic reactions and gene programs become possible when this compound is present?" The mechanism is enabling, not triggering.
</Callout>
