Copper is one of the most tightly regulated metals in the human body. That regulation exists because free copper is dangerous — Cu²⁺ in solution catalyzes the Fenton-like reaction that converts hydrogen peroxide into hydroxyl radicals, one of the most reactive species in biochemistry. The cell keeps copper under strict control: shuttled between dedicated chaperone proteins, almost none circulates in free ionic form. The tissue concentration of free copper is effectively zero under normal conditions.

Yet copper is also essential. A meaningful set of enzymes require a copper ion at their active site to function at all. When copper availability drops, those enzymes slow or stop. The consequence isn't subtle — you can trace copper deficiency to specific enzyme failures and specific tissue effects.

## Copper-requiring enzymes and why they matter for tissue repair

The key enzymes and their functions:

**Lysyl oxidase (LOX)** catalyzes the crosslinking of collagen and elastin fibers. This is the step that converts newly synthesized collagen and elastin from soluble, weak precursors into the insoluble, mechanically strong matrix structures they're supposed to be. Without copper, lysyl oxidase doesn't function. Without lysyl oxidase, collagen fibers don't crosslink. The result is structurally defective matrix — like having bricks without mortar.

**Superoxide dismutase 1 (SOD1)** is the primary cytoplasmic antioxidant enzyme responsible for neutralizing superoxide radicals. It requires both copper and zinc at its active site. SOD1 is especially relevant to wound healing, where oxidative stress is high and antioxidant capacity directly determines how much bystander tissue damage accumulates.

**Cytochrome c oxidase** is the terminal enzyme of the mitochondrial electron transport chain — the step where electron flow is coupled to ATP synthesis. It's a copper-containing protein. Copper deficiency slows cellular energy production at the most fundamental level.

Other copper-dependent enzymes include dopamine beta-hydroxylase (converts dopamine to norepinephrine) and ceruloplasmin (iron metabolism, acute-phase response). These are biologically real but less relevant to the tissue repair context this course focuses on.

<Callout type="key" title="Lysyl oxidase is the most mechanistically relevant enzyme here">
For tissue repair and skin quality, lysyl oxidase is the copper-requiring enzyme that matters most. Collagen crosslinking is the final step in converting new collagen synthesis into actual structural strength. A cell that's producing collagen but doesn't have the copper to run lysyl oxidase is producing collagen it can't crosslink — and uncrosslinked collagen is functionally inferior. This is why copper deficiency selectively weakens connective tissue even when protein synthesis continues normally.
</Callout>

## The chelation problem, and why GHK-Cu solves it

Getting copper into cells and to copper-requiring enzymes requires solving two problems simultaneously:

**Problem 1: Free copper is toxic.** Free Cu²⁺ drives oxidative damage. Simply delivering copper ions would cause local tissue damage before any enzyme benefit occurred.

**Problem 2: Copper chelated too tightly is inert.** A copper ion bound so strongly that it can't be released is biologically useless — you'd be delivering a molecule that holds copper without ever transferring it.

GHK's chelation resolves both problems. The copper is bound tightly enough to suppress its free-radical activity — the chelated complex doesn't catalyze the Fenton-like reaction that free Cu²⁺ does. But the binding is not so tight that the copper can't be transferred to a copper-requiring enzyme or chaperone protein with higher affinity. The result is copper that is safely transported but bioavailable for transfer — a controlled delivery vehicle, not a permanent trap.

The stability constant of the GHK-Cu complex has been measured, and it falls in the range that allows this: stable against free-radical activity, labile enough for enzymatic transfer. This is not a unique property of GHK — it's a property of copper-histidine complexes generally, and GHK happens to contain histidine as its central amino acid with the imidazole nitrogen as the primary copper anchor.

## What is and isn't proven about the delivery chain

This is where calibrated confidence matters.

**What is established:** GHK chelates Cu²⁺. The complex is stable and non-toxic in the range relevant to its biological effects. GHK-Cu increases collagen synthesis and promotes wound healing in cell culture and animal models. Copper is a required cofactor for lysyl oxidase. Lysyl oxidase activity correlates with collagen matrix quality.

**What is inferred, not directly proven:** The specific claim that GHK-Cu delivers copper directly to lysyl oxidase at the mechanistic level — that the copper leaves GHK and enters LOX's active site — has not been demonstrated biochemically. The chain from "GHK-Cu present" to "lysyl oxidase active" to "collagen crosslinking improved" is consistent with available evidence and mechanistically plausible, but the intermediate step of copper transfer to LOX specifically has not been isolated and confirmed experimentally.

This distinction matters. "GHK-Cu improves collagen synthesis and matrix quality, consistent with copper delivery to lysyl oxidase" is what the evidence supports. "GHK-Cu delivers copper to lysyl oxidase" stated as a biochemically established fact is one inference step ahead of the evidence.

<Callout type="caution" title="How to hold this accurately">
The copper delivery mechanism is the most plausible explanation for GHK-Cu's effects on collagen and connective tissue. It is well-supported by indirect evidence and mechanistically sound. What it is not is a directly demonstrated biochemical reaction the way, for example, actin-sequestration by Tβ4 has been demonstrated. When discussing the mechanism, the accurate frame is: "the effects are consistent with copper delivery to copper-requiring enzymes, particularly lysyl oxidase" — not "we know it delivers copper to lysyl oxidase."
</Callout>
