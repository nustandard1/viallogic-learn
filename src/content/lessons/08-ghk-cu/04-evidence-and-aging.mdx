GHK-Cu has something relatively rare in this course's compound list: actual human trial data. The route of administration matters enormously, though. The topical evidence and the injectable evidence are in completely different positions, and conflating them is a common mistake when discussing this compound.

## Topical evidence: the strongest position

Topical GHK-Cu has been studied in human subjects for skin applications — wrinkle reduction, skin thickness, firmness, and related endpoints. Several randomized controlled trials have demonstrated statistically significant improvements in skin quality metrics with topical GHK-Cu compared to vehicle control.

The mechanisms align with what we'd expect from the copper-delivery and collagen-synthesis story: increased collagen density, improved skin texture, reduction in fine lines. The topical route of administration also makes mechanistic sense — GHK-Cu doesn't need to survive systemic circulation or cross cell membranes in the same way an injected compound does. It can act locally in the dermis where fibroblasts, collagen, and copper-requiring enzymes all reside.

This is the evidence base that most legitimately supports enthusiasm for GHK-Cu. Controlled human studies, replicated effects, plausible mechanism. The limitations: effect sizes are modest (real improvements, not transformations), these are cosmetic endpoint studies (not wound healing or systemic aging studies), and they don't tell us what injectable GHK-Cu does in vivo.

## Injectable evidence: thin and largely animal-based

The injectable evidence for GHK-Cu is sparse. What exists is primarily:

- Animal wound healing studies showing accelerated closure and improved tissue organization
- Animal hair growth studies (GHK-Cu in follicle models and rodent hair growth assays)
- Cell culture mechanistic work (collagen upregulation, decorin, antioxidant gene programs)

What is absent: human clinical trials using injectable GHK-Cu. The dose for subcutaneous or intramuscular injection in humans has not been established in any clinical study. Animal model doses don't reliably translate across species for peptide compounds. Anyone using injectable GHK-Cu for systemic repair or anti-aging effects is operating in essentially uncharted territory regarding dose-response in humans.

<Callout type="caution" title="Route of administration changes the evidence base entirely">
A human RCT on topical GHK-Cu does not support claims about injectable GHK-Cu. The compound's behavior under the two routes is different: bioavailability, tissue distribution, local vs. systemic concentration, and metabolism all change with route. "GHK-Cu has human trial evidence" is accurate only for topical; stating it in a context that implies injectable use has human support is a significant mischaracterization of the evidence.
</Callout>

## The aging connection and plasma concentration narrative

The most frequently cited GHK framing in anti-aging contexts is a plasma concentration decline with age: GHK is present at high concentrations in young plasma (often cited as ~200 ng/mL at age 20) and falls substantially by age 60 (often cited as ~80 ng/mL). The implication is that this decline contributes to aging, and that supplementing GHK-Cu can reverse it.

This narrative has a significant source problem. The plasma concentration numbers originate from Loren Pickart's 1973 dissertation and a small number of related early publications. Pickart is the primary researcher behind GHK-Cu — he identified the compound, named it, and has been its most prominent proponent for five decades. The original concentration measurements have not been independently replicated in the peer-reviewed literature at the scale required to establish them as a reliable biological fact.

This doesn't mean the numbers are wrong. It means they rest on a single-source foundation from the 1970s using measurement methods that predate modern mass spectrometry-based quantification. The narrative built on them — that GHK decline is a driver of aging — is a hypothesis, not an established finding.

<Callout type="info" title="Holding the plasma decline story accurately">
"GHK plasma concentrations may decline with age, with one early study reporting a drop from roughly 200 to 80 ng/mL" is accurate framing. "GHK levels decline with age and this decline drives aging" promotes a hypothesis to a conclusion. The distinction matters for how you communicate the aging relevance of this compound — interesting hypothesis, limited direct evidence.
</Callout>

## How to hold the evidence overall

| Application | Evidence quality | Human data? |
|---|---|---|
| Topical skin quality | Moderate — multiple human RCTs | Yes |
| Wound healing (topical) | Moderate — controlled animal studies, some human data | Limited |
| Hair follicle effects | Low-moderate — animal and cell culture | No RCT |
| Injectable repair/anti-aging | Low — animal models only | No |
| Plasma GHK decline → aging | Hypothesis — single early source | Observational, not replicated |

The strongest evidence in this module is for topical use on skin quality — this is unusually solid for a non-approved compound in this space. The injectable evidence is at the level of "animal data support investigation," not "established clinical effect." The aging narrative is a mechanistically coherent hypothesis that has not been confirmed.

<Checkpoint number={4}>
<span slot="question">Why can't the topical GHK-Cu human trial evidence be applied to justify injectable use, and what is the honest framing for the plasma decline narrative?</span>

Topical and injectable GHK-Cu differ fundamentally in bioavailability, tissue distribution, and the concentrations reached at target sites. A topical RCT shows what happens when GHK-Cu is applied to skin at dermis-level concentrations — local effects on local fibroblasts and matrix. An injectable compound must survive systemic circulation, distributes differently, reaches different tissues at different concentrations, and hasn't been studied in human subjects at any dose. The mechanisms overlap but the evidence doesn't transfer between routes. On the plasma decline narrative: the reported decline from ~200 to ~80 ng/mL with age originates from Loren Pickart's 1973 dissertation and has not been independently replicated using modern quantitative methods. The numbers may be accurate, but they rest on a single source. The claim that this decline drives aging is a hypothesis built on those numbers — coherent and worth investigating, but not an established finding. Accurate framing: GHK plasma levels may decline with age per early data; whether this decline contributes causally to aging is unknown.
</Checkpoint>

This module's evidence map is one of the more nuanced in the course: a genuine topical win, a thin injectable story, and an aging narrative that is interesting but needs its source problem acknowledged. The compound is real and the mechanism is real — the evidence hierarchy just requires you to be honest about which parts are solid and which are still being built.
