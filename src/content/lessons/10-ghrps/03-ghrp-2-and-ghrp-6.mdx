Moving from <Term id="ipamorelin">ipamorelin</Term> toward less selective <Term id="ghrp">GHRPs</Term> means accepting a trade. The compounds in this lesson — <Term id="ghrp-2">GHRP-2</Term> and <Term id="ghrp-6">GHRP-6</Term> — produce more GH per dose than ipamorelin in most direct comparisons. They do this by more broadly activating <Term id="ghs-r1a">GHS-R1a</Term> signaling, which also means more activation of the pathways that drive <Term id="cortisol">cortisol</Term> and <Term id="prolactin">prolactin</Term> elevation. Understanding this trade requires understanding that the side effects aren't manufacturing contamination or dosing errors — they're mechanistically built into what these compounds are.

## GHRP-2

GHRP-2 is a synthetic hexapeptide (six amino acids) with a GH-stimulating potency clearly above ipamorelin. In human studies, it produces consistent, dose-dependent GH pulses, and the GH output per injection is meaningful. The mechanism is the same as all GHRPs: <Term id="ghs-r1a">GHS-R1a</Term> → Gq/<Term id="plc">PLC</Term> → <Term id="ip3">IP3</Term> → calcium spike → <Term id="exocytosis">exocytosis</Term> of stored GH.

What GHRP-2 activates more broadly than ipamorelin:

**Cortisol:** GHRP-2 stimulates ACTH release from the anterior pituitary more significantly than ipamorelin. ACTH drives cortisol production from the adrenal glands. In human pharmacokinetic studies, GHRP-2 administration produces measurable cortisol elevation — not huge, but consistent. Across multiple daily injections over weeks, this pattern is worth factoring in for anyone using GHRPs in a body-composition context, where cortisol's catabolic and anti-insulin effects work against the anabolic goals GH is being used to support.

**Prolactin:** GHRP-2 also elevates prolactin more than ipamorelin. Mechanistically, prolactin is secreted by lactotroph cells in the anterior pituitary in response to stimuli including some GHS-R1a signaling. As noted in Lesson 2, the primary relevance for most male users is prolactin's capacity to suppress LH and FSH signaling, which reduces testosterone production. The effect per dose is modest but present.

Neither effect is a disqualifying flaw. Many researchers use GHRP-2 specifically because the stronger GH pulse is the priority, and the cortisol/prolactin effects are managed or accepted. The point is to know what you're getting.

<Callout type="info" title="GHRP-2 has human pharmacokinetic data">
GHRP-2 was studied in early-phase clinical trials during the 1990s and 2000s. It was investigated for GH deficiency applications and produced documented GH responses in human subjects. Like ipamorelin, it never received FDA approval, but its PK profile in humans is characterized more thoroughly than many research peptides. This puts it in the same evidence tier as ipamorelin: mechanism confirmed in humans, no approved indication, no long-term safety data in healthy adults.
</Callout>

## GHRP-6

GHRP-6 is a hexapeptide structurally closer to native <Term id="ghrelin">ghrelin</Term> than ipamorelin or GHRP-2. That structural similarity is precisely why GHRP-6 produces something the other compounds largely don't: pronounced appetite stimulation.

Ghrelin's role as a hunger signal runs through GHS-R1a expressed in the hypothalamus — specifically the arcuate nucleus and other appetite-regulating regions. When GHRP-6 activates GHS-R1a, it does so in a way that engages this hypothalamic pathway meaningfully. The result is a noticeable increase in appetite within 30–60 minutes of injection, peaking as GH levels rise. This is not an incidental side effect — it's the ghrelin pathway doing exactly what the ghrelin pathway does.

For users who need to eat in a caloric surplus (hypertrophy protocols, recovery from illness, hard-gaining athletes), this appetite drive can be a feature. GHRP-6 is sometimes deliberately chosen for this reason — the compound both stimulates GH and makes consuming the caloric load required to support muscle growth considerably easier.

For users in a deficit or those managing weight while seeking body composition improvements, GHRP-6's appetite stimulation is a problem. The hunger signal can be strong enough to undermine caloric targets.

GHRP-6 also elevates cortisol and prolactin — similar to GHRP-2, and in most comparisons modestly more so.

<Callout type="caution" title="The appetite effect is dose-dependent and real">
GHRP-6's appetite stimulation is not a minor nuance — at typical research doses (100–200 mcg per injection), users consistently report significant hunger within an hour. This is well-documented in the literature and widely reported in practitioner experience. If appetite management matters for your protocol goals, this is a meaningful compound characteristic, not a background concern.
</Callout>

## The selectivity spectrum so far

At this point in the module, the progression is clear:

| Compound | GH Pulse | Cortisol | Prolactin | Appetite |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ipamorelin | Moderate | Minimal | Minimal | None |
| GHRP-2 | Strong | Moderate | Moderate | Minimal |
| GHRP-6 | Strong | Moderate-High | Moderate | Significant |

The frame isn't "which compound is best" — it's "what trade are you making." Ipamorelin is the cleanest; GHRP-2 gives you more GH with some cortisol/prolactin cost; GHRP-6 gives you similar GH to GHRP-2 plus genuine appetite stimulation.

Where ipamorelin's selective GH pulse makes sense: protocols where minimizing cortisol exposure matters, anyone who has other reasons to manage prolactin carefully, or researchers who want to isolate the GH variable.

Where GHRP-2 makes sense: when stronger GH output is the priority and the cortisol/prolactin trade is acceptable, especially in shorter-term protocols where cumulative effects are less of a concern.

Where GHRP-6 makes sense: when appetite stimulation is a genuine goal alongside GH output, or when caloric surplus support is as important as the GH effect itself.

<Checkpoint number={1}>
<span slot="question">A researcher argues that GHRP-6's appetite stimulation is just a side effect that can be managed with willpower, and the stronger GH pulse is what matters. What's the problem with this framing — and what does it get right?</span>

The framing gets one thing right: GHRP-6 does produce a meaningful GH pulse, and for specific use cases the appetite effect can align with protocol goals rather than fight them. But calling it "just a side effect manageable with willpower" misses why the effect exists. GHRP-6's appetite stimulation runs through the same GHS-R1a signaling in the hypothalamic arcuate nucleus that ghrelin uses to signal genuine hunger — it is a pharmacological hunger signal, not a mild preference for snacks. The intensity is dose-dependent and in many users strong enough to make caloric control genuinely difficult, particularly in a deficit. More precisely: the appetite effect and the GH effect are both coming from the same receptor pathway. You cannot selectively suppress one without selectively targeting the hypothalamic vs. pituitary expression sites, which GHRP-6 does not do. The compound activates both. Willpower can partially compensate, but framing a strong pharmacological appetite drive as something "manageable" understates its practical impact for anyone whose protocol goal involves caloric restriction.
</Checkpoint>
