<Term id="retatrutide">Retatrutide</Term> is a 41-amino acid peptide that activates three receptors: the GLP-1 receptor, the GIP receptor, and the <Term id="glucagon-receptor">glucagon receptor</Term>. It's being developed by Eli Lilly — the same company behind tirzepatide — under the TRIUMPH Phase 3 trial program. Phase 2 results published in 2023 showed mean weight reduction of approximately 24.2% at the highest dose (12 mg) over 48 weeks, with roughly 26% of participants achieving ≥30% body weight loss. These numbers, if they replicate in Phase 3, would represent the highest weight reduction from any pharmacological intervention outside of surgery.

The question this lesson answers is not "does adding glucagon do more?" — the Phase 2 answer is clearly yes. The question is: what is the glucagon receptor doing here, and why would activating a hormone classically associated with raising blood glucose help with metabolic disease?

## What glucagon does — and why it's counterintuitive

<Term id="glucagon">Glucagon</Term> is a 29-amino acid peptide produced by alpha cells in the pancreatic islets. Its canonical role is the counter-regulatory hormone to insulin: when blood glucose drops, glucagon is released and drives glycogenolysis in the liver (breaking down glycogen to release glucose) and gluconeogenesis (synthesizing new glucose from amino acids and lactate). It raises blood glucose. From a metabolic disease standpoint, this sounds like exactly the wrong receptor to activate when you're trying to treat obesity and type 2 diabetes.

But glucagon does more than raise blood glucose. The glucagon receptor is also expressed in adipose tissue, the liver, and most importantly for this application, in brown adipose tissue (BAT) — the thermogenic fat depot that burns calories to generate heat rather than storing them. Glucagon receptor activation in BAT and white adipose tissue drives:

- **Lipolysis** — breakdown of stored triglycerides into free fatty acids, directly mobilizing fat from adipose stores
- **Thermogenesis** — increased energy expenditure through heat generation in BAT, independent of food intake
- **Hepatic fat clearance** — reduced liver fat content, relevant for the non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) component of metabolic syndrome

The net effect is that glucagon receptor agonism adds an energy expenditure component to the equation — something GLP-1 and GIP don't provide directly. GLP-1 and GIP work primarily by reducing energy intake (appetite suppression, slower gastric emptying). Glucagon receptor activation can increase energy expenditure even in the absence of eating less.

<Callout type="key" title="The two-lever model">
The GLP-1/GIP combination reduces the energy in side of the equation — less appetite, smaller meals, slower gastric emptying. The glucagon receptor adds leverage on the energy out side — more calories burned through lipolysis and thermogenesis. In theory, pulling both levers simultaneously should produce larger weight loss than pulling either alone, and the Phase 2 data is consistent with that prediction. The practical tradeoff: glucagon also raises blood glucose, meaning the insulinotropic components (GLP-1 and GIP) must be active enough to offset the glucagon-driven hyperglycemia. Retatrutide's receptor-activation balance is designed to keep the glucose outcomes acceptable — the Phase 2 HbA1c data confirmed it doesn't significantly worsen glycemic control.
</Callout>

## The Phase 2 data — and what Phase 3 still needs to establish

The Phase 2 trial randomized 338 participants without diabetes to retatrutide 1, 4, 8, or 12 mg weekly or placebo for 48 weeks. Mean weight reductions were roughly 17.5%, 22.8%, and 24.2% at the 4, 8, and 12 mg doses respectively — dose-dependent and substantially exceeding tirzepatide's Phase 3 results at equivalent timepoints.

What Phase 2 cannot establish: cardiovascular outcomes. The TRIUMPH Phase 3 program will include large cardiovascular outcomes trials analogous to SUSTAIN-6 and SELECT — but those results are years away. The STEP and SURMOUNT programs ran 60,000+ person-years of exposure across their trial portfolios before the SELECT cardiovascular outcomes data was available. Retatrutide has a fraction of that exposure history. Whether the cardiovascular outcome benefit seen with semaglutide and (expected with) tirzepatide extends to triple agonism remains to be shown.

<Callout type="info" title="Side effects — is the extra mechanism also extra nausea?">
Phase 2 GI side effects with retatrutide were broadly similar to tirzepatide — nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea as the most common, with rates highest during dose escalation and declining at steady state. No new safety signal emerged from the glucagon receptor component that wasn't attributable to the GLP-1/GIP mechanisms already familiar from the prior drugs. This is reassuring but provisional: Phase 2 sample sizes aren't designed to detect rare safety events, and the longer the drug is in broader use, the more complete the safety picture becomes.
</Callout>

## The 41-amino acid problem

Retatrutide is 41 amino acids long. That one amino acid above the FDA's 40-amino acid regulatory threshold — the line that separates "small molecule drug" from "biologic" — has significant consequences.

Biologics face a separate approval pathway (BLA, not NDA), different manufacturing standards, and different post-market exclusivity rules. The critical commercial implication is Medicare drug price negotiation. Under the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), small molecule drugs become eligible for Medicare price negotiation after 9 years of market exclusivity; biologics get 13 years. Semaglutide (a 26-amino acid peptide) and tirzepatide (39 amino acids) are technically small molecules under this definition. If retatrutide is classified as a biologic, Eli Lilly gets 4 additional years of negotiation-protected pricing — worth potentially tens of billions of dollars in cumulative revenue.

There is active legal and regulatory dispute about whether amino acid count alone should determine biologic status for peptides of this length, or whether the regulatory definition should track with the functional characteristics of the molecule. The FDA has historically been inconsistent on this classification for peptides in the 40–50 amino acid range. The outcome of that dispute will shape both retatrutide's access economics and set precedent for the next generation of even larger engineered peptides.

<Callout type="caution" title="Where the evidence stands">
Retatrutide has no FDA approval as of this writing. The Phase 2 weight loss signal is real and striking, but Phase 2 is designed to establish dose-response and early safety signals — not to confirm efficacy in the definitively powered populations that Phase 3 provides. The triple agonism rationale is mechanistically coherent. The regulatory path is contested. The cardiovascular outcomes data doesn't exist yet. This is a compound worth following closely; it is not a compound with the clinical evidence profile of semaglutide or tirzepatide yet.
</Callout>
