## The module in one paragraph

GLP-1 is a gut-derived incretin hormone with a 2-minute native half-life — destroyed by DPP-4 before it can be therapeutically useful. Two engineering moves (DPP-4-resistant amino acid substitution + lipidation for albumin binding) convert it into a once-weekly drug. The GLP-1 receptor is a Gs-coupled GPCR expressed in the pancreas, hypothalamus, heart, kidney, and elsewhere; its insulin-stimulating effect is glucose-dependent, which is why hypoglycemia is not a meaningful risk at therapeutic doses. Semaglutide (GLP-1 monoagonist) produces ~15% weight loss and cardiovascular outcome benefits that exceed what weight reduction alone would predict — suggesting direct cardiac GLP-1R signaling independent of metabolic improvement. Tirzepatide (dual GLP-1R/GIPR agonist) produces ~21% weight loss; the GIP receptor's contribution is paradoxical — GIP physiologically promotes fat storage in the postprandial state, but sustained pharmacological GIP receptor activation appears to shift adipocyte behavior in the opposite direction, a mechanism still actively debated. The CNS GIP receptor contribution to satiety is likely more established. Retatrutide (triple GLP-1R/GIPR/glucagon receptor agonist) adds a third mechanism — glucagon receptor activation in adipose and brown fat drives lipolysis and thermogenesis (energy expenditure), providing a lever on the output side of the energy equation that the first two receptors don't directly address. Phase 2 data shows ~24% weight loss; Phase 3 is ongoing; cardiovascular outcomes data does not yet exist. Its 41-amino acid structure puts it one residue above the FDA threshold for biologic vs. drug classification, with major pricing and access implications under the Inflation Reduction Act. All three drugs share GI side effects (nausea, vomiting) tied to gastric slowing, and a lean mass loss concern proportional to the degree of weight reduction.
