Most peptide information people encounter doesn't come from journals. It comes from Instagram posts, YouTube videos, Reddit threads, and TikToks — usually several steps removed from whatever research originally inspired the claim. By the time a rodent finding becomes a caption, it's often been stripped of its caveats, amplified by financial incentives, and presented as settled fact.

This module builds three connected skills: understanding where evidence actually sits on the quality spectrum, evaluating whether that evidence is being represented honestly, and verifying that the compound you're looking at is what it claims to be.

## What this module covers

- **The evidence hierarchy** — why a rat study and an RCT are not the same kind of claim, and where popular peptides actually sit on the spectrum
- **Reading research critically** — sample size, funding, blinding, publication bias, statistical vs clinical significance
- **Decoding claims** — how findings travel from source to caption, the red flag phrases that signal an overclaim, and a five-question framework for evaluating anything you see online
- **What a COA actually contains** — and how to tell a real one from a vendor-produced PDF

## How to use this module

Four lessons, each short and practical. Lessons 1 and 2 build the research literacy foundation. Lesson 3 is the most immediately applicable — it's the framework you'll use every time you encounter a peptide claim on social media. Lesson 4 shifts to product quality, because evidence quality doesn't matter if the compound itself isn't what it claims to be.

The Dr. Harmon interview for this module is more applied than previous ones — expect to walk through a specific claim and a COA scenario.
