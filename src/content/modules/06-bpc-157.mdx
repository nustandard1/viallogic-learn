BPC-157 is the most widely studied synthetic peptide in the tissue repair category — over 200 published animal studies, a phase 2 human trial in inflammatory bowel disease, and a reputation that spans tendon injuries, gut healing, and CNS effects. It is also one of the most mischaracterized compounds in the space.

The most common description — "anti-inflammatory" — is technically incomplete and mechanistically backwards. BPC-157 doesn't primarily work by suppressing inflammation. It works by actively driving the vascular and cellular processes that rebuild tissue. Reduced inflammation is what happens when repair progresses. That distinction matters because it predicts when to use it, what to expect from it, and how to read the evidence honestly.

## What this module covers

- **What BPC-157 actually is** — its origin from human gastric protein, why "endogenous" is an imprecise label, what the research landscape looks like, and the Pliva trial history
- **The mechanisms** — why there's no confirmed receptor, the three parallel downstream pathways (eNOS/NO, VEGF, FAK-paxillin), and the L-NAME pharmacological proof
- **Tendon and tissue healing** — why tendons are the test case, how BPC-157's mechanisms map directly onto tendon repair's specific bottlenecks, and the growth hormone receptor finding
- **Systemic effects and CNS** — the biological switch that explains lasting effects from a short-lived peptide, route rationale, and CNS effects in animal models
