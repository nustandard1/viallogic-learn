BPC-157 is the most widely studied synthetic peptide in the tissue repair category — over 200 published animal studies, a phase 2 human trial in inflammatory bowel disease, and a reputation that spans tendon injuries, gut healing, and CNS effects simultaneously. It is also one of the most mischaracterized compounds in the space.

The most common description — "anti-inflammatory" — is technically true but mechanistically backwards. BPC-157 doesn't primarily work by suppressing inflammation. It works by actively driving the vascular and cellular processes that rebuild tissue. Reduced inflammation is what happens when repair progresses. That distinction matters because it predicts when to use it, what to combine it with, and what evidence should and shouldn't persuade you.

## What this module covers

- **What BPC-157 actually is** — its origin from human gastric protein, why "endogenous" is an imprecise label, and what the research landscape actually looks like
- **The nitric oxide mechanism** — eNOS upregulation, what NO does for vasculature and repair, and the L-NAME pharmacological proof that this is the primary driver
- **Tendon and tissue healing** — why tendons are the test case, what fibroblast and tenocyte activation means in practice, and how to read the animal model evidence correctly
- **Systemic effects and CNS** — the paradox of systemic effects from a short-half-life peptide, route rationale, and GABAergic and dopaminergic effects in animal models

## How to use this module

Four lessons. The first two are mechanistic foundations — you need the nitric oxide picture before tendon healing makes sense. Lesson 3 connects everything back to Module 3's healing cascade. Lesson 4 covers the two most debated aspects of BPC-157.

The Dr. Harmon interview will ask you to walk through the mechanism from the bottom up — starting with eNOS, not with the outcome — and to apply the evidence evaluation framework from Module 5. Both are worth preparing.
