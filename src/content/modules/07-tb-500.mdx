Thymosin Beta-4 — the peptide that TB-500 is derived from — is one of the most abundant peptides in virtually every cell in your body. It's not a synthetic research construct derived from a fragment of a gastric protein. It's a naturally produced, regulated signaling molecule with identified receptors and well-characterized functions. The contrast with BPC-157 is worth holding onto: "endogenous" means something different here.

The research peptide market sells it under the name TB-500 — a vendor name, not a scientific one. The science uses Thymosin Beta-4, abbreviated Tβ4. Most of the published research is on Tβ4, not "TB-500" specifically. That distinction matters when you go looking for evidence.

## What this module covers

- **What TB-500 actually is** — the Tβ4 relationship, why the endogenous label is accurate here (and what that does and doesn't mean), and the research landscape
- **The mechanism** — actin sequestration explained accessibly, how it drives cell motility, stem cell mobilization, and the anti-inflammatory component
- **Tissue healing and distribution** — the systemic vs. local distinction, evidence by context (cardiac, wound, musculoskeletal), and reading the evidence honestly
- **The BPC-157 pairing** — why these two compounds address different parts of the repair problem and why the combination is mechanistically coherent

## How to use this module

Four lessons plus a recap. Lessons 1–3 build the foundation: what it is, how the mechanism works, and what the evidence shows. Lesson 4 is the payoff — the BPC-157 synergy rationale — which only makes sense once you understand both compounds.
