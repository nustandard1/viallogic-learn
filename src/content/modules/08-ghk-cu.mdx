GHK-Cu is mechanistically unlike anything covered so far in this course. BPC-157 engages downstream signaling without a confirmed receptor. TB-500 directly binds actin. GHK-Cu does neither — it primarily works by delivering a metal cofactor that enzymes need and by modulating gene transcription programs broadly. The question isn't "what receptor does it hit?" It's "what becomes possible when copper is properly available and certain gene programs are active?"

That shift in framing is the first thing this module teaches. The second is how to read a mechanistic claim carefully — because GHK-Cu has attracted some of the most impressive-sounding mechanism language in the research peptide space, and much of it requires scrutiny.

## What this module covers

- **What GHK-Cu is** — the tripeptide, its genuine endogenous origin from SPARC cleavage, the GHK vs. GHK-Cu distinction, and how it differs from every other compound in this course
- **The copper mechanism** — why copper is biologically essential, how GHK chelates it safely, and what the copper-delivery-to-enzymes chain actually says and doesn't say
- **The gene expression story** — what the Connectivity Map analysis actually found, how to read it honestly, and why "resetting the genome" is marketing language, not a scientific conclusion
- **Evidence and the aging connection** — where the evidence is genuinely strong (topical), where it's thin (injectable), and how to hold the plasma decline narrative accurately

## How to use this module

Four lessons plus a recap. Lessons 1–2 build the mechanistic foundation. Lesson 3 is the most epistemically demanding — it requires applying the Module 5 evidence framework directly to a specific claim. Lesson 4 puts it all in context. The Dr. Harmon interview will ask you to explain the mechanism, characterize the evidence, and apply the right level of confidence to specific claims.
