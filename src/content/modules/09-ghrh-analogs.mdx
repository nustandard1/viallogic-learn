Module 4 covered the GH axis from hypothalamus to IGF-1 — the full regulatory cascade that governs growth hormone output. This module is where that foundation pays off. GHRH analogs are the compounds that plug directly into Step 1 of that axis: they bind the GHRH receptor on pituitary somatotroph cells and tell them to release GH. All three compounds in this module work that way. What differs — dramatically — is how long they occupy that receptor.

That pharmacokinetic difference is not a minor detail. The gap between a compound with a 15-minute half-life and one with a 7-day half-life is the difference between firing a physiologic pulse and running a near-continuous infusion. Those produce different GH profiles, different receptor dynamics, and different long-term effects on axis responsiveness.

## What this module covers

- **The GHRH receptor and why native GHRH fails as a drug** — the mechanism all three analogs share, and the specific problem each one is engineered to solve
- **Sermorelin** — the original 29-amino acid fragment, its FDA history, and why its short half-life is a feature rather than a limitation
- **CJC-1295 with DAC vs. Mod GRF 1-29** — the DAC mechanism, the dramatic half-life gap, and the mislabeling problem that makes this the most practically confusing compound pair in the category
- **Tesamorelin** — the only FDA-approved compound in this group, what the clinical trial data actually established, and what it doesn't tell you about off-label use
