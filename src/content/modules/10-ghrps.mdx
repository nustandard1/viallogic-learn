Module 9 covered the GHRH analogs — compounds that bind the GHRH receptor on pituitary somatotroph cells and prime the axis to release GH. This module covers the other half of the GH secretagogue picture: GHRPs, which hit a completely different receptor via a completely different signaling cascade to produce GH release through a different mechanism entirely.

That distinction is not a technical footnote. It's why these two compound families are synergistic rather than redundant — and it's the foundational concept this module builds on.

## What this module covers

- **The ghrelin receptor and Gq/calcium signaling** — the mechanism all four GHRPs share, and why it complements the Gs/cAMP pathway of GHRH analogs rather than duplicating it
- **Ipamorelin** — what makes it the "clean" GHRP, what biased agonism actually means here, and why it became the default starting point for most GH secretagogue protocols
- **GHRP-2 and GHRP-6** — more potent GH release, but at the cost of cortisol, prolactin, and in GHRP-6's case, appetite stimulation that's mechanistically inseparable from the compound's structure
- **Hexarelin** — the most potent GHRP in the group, the fastest to desensitize, and the one that binds a second receptor (CD36) with a cardioprotective mechanism that has nothing to do with GH

## How to use this module

Four lessons plus a recap. Module 4 (the GH axis) and Module 9 (GHRH analogs) are both prerequisite here — this module assumes you understand somatotroph biology, pulsatility, desensitization, and the GHRH receptor mechanism. If either is fuzzy, reviewing Module 4's pulsatility lesson and Module 9's first lesson before starting here will make the synergy concept immediately clear.

The Dr. Harmon interview will push on the selectivity spectrum, what "clean" actually means mechanistically, and the hexarelin story — particularly whether more potency is ever actually better.
